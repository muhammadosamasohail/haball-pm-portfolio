# Market Research Summary
## Alternative-Signal Underwriting for Thin-File Distributors

**Author:** Muhammad Osama Sohail  **Date:** 2026-07-08  **Status:** Draft, for portfolio purposes

### 1. Market Context

Pakistan's economy runs heavily on informal activity. The informal economy is roughly 40% of GDP and 72% of employment (source: desk research). At the retail and distribution layer, roughly 90% of retailers and distributors have zero formal digital transaction history (source: desk research). Formal credit reaches very few businesses: traditional lenders leave about 94% of SMEs without formal credit access, and only around 200,000 of an estimated 3.2 million SMEs have any formal bank credit relationship (source: desk research).

Layered on top of this is FBR's mandatory e-invoicing rollout (effective Nov 2025), which imposes real upfront cost on small merchants, roughly PKR 100,000 to 500,000, with penalties up to 2% of transaction value for non-compliance (source: desk research). This pulls in two directions at once: it pushes merchants toward digitization through the mandate, while the upfront cost pushes the smallest merchants further from it in the short term.

### 2. Why This Matters to Haball Specifically

Wisaaq is built to finance exactly the distributors most likely to fall in the no-file majority. That is Wisaaq's underwriting bottleneck: the product's target users are, by design, the people conventional credit scoring cannot assess. Haball's position is unusual because it also operates the rails that generate behavioral data on these same distributors, Blink for payments and orders and Wasal for invoicing and FBR registration. The starting hypothesis is that this combination lets Haball see repayment-relevant behavior that a standalone lender cannot, which is precisely the gap that keeps 94% of SMEs outside formal credit.

### 3. Competitive / Comparative Note

Using alternative data (payment behavior, transaction patterns, invoice history) to assess thin-file borrowers is a known approach in emerging-market fintech, not a novel invention. Variations appear across supply-chain finance and merchant-lending globally. What is harder to verify from the outside is exactly who is doing this for Pakistani distributors at Haball's specific position in the supply chain; that space appears under-documented publicly, so I am not naming specific local competitors I cannot confirm. The honest read is that the general pattern is established, and the specific application to Haball's three-product ecosystem is where any distinctiveness would lie.

### 4. Implication for Product Strategy

The starting takeaway, held loosely: Haball's edge here is likely less about inventing a scoring technique and more about owning the data-generation layer that makes thin-file underwriting possible at all. If that holds, the strategic question is less "what model do we build" and more "how do we grow Blink and Wasal adoption so more distributors become underwritable over time." Both points would need validation with Haball's risk and data teams before being treated as more than a hypothesis.
